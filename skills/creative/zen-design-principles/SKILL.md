# Zen Design Principles

Synthesized from Garr Reynolds' *Presentation Zen*, Zen aesthetics, and proven design practice.

## The Seven Core Principles

### 1. Kanso (簡素) — Simplicity

> "If everything is important, then nothing is important."

**Rule:** Eliminate until only the essential remains. Not dumbing down — distilling.

**In practice:**
- One core message per slide / page / view
- No decorative elements that don't serve the message
- Bullet points are usually a failure of design — replace with a single idea + visual
- If you can remove it and the message still works, remove it

### 2. Shizen (自然) — Naturalness

> Design should feel inevitable, not forced.

**Rule:** The design should feel like it grew there. Avoid arbitrary choices.

**In practice:**
- Don't force symmetry when asymmetry feels right
- Let the content dictate the layout, not the template
- Avoid over-styling — no 3D effects, gratuitous gradients, clip art
- Natural imagery > staged stock photos

### 3. Shibumi (渋み) — Elegant Restraint

> Understated excellence. Articulate brevity.

**Rule:** Elegance emerges from what you withhold, not what you add.

**In practice:**
- Maximum impact with minimum elements
- One typeface family (weight variation for hierarchy)
- Limited color palette (2-3 colors + neutrals)
- If it feels "designed," you've gone too far

### 4. Ma (間) — Empty Space

> "White space is not wasted space — it's the breathing room that amplifies what remains."

**Rule:** Empty space is an active design element, not a void to fill.

**In practice:**
- Give elements room to breathe — generous margins and padding
- One idea with space around it hits harder than three ideas crammed together
- Use the Rule of Thirds for placement
- Empty 60-70% of the canvas. What remains will be seen.

### 5. Signal-to-Noise Ratio

> Maximize the message. Minimize everything else.

**Rule:** Every pixel that isn't the message is noise competing with it.

**In practice:**
- Kill logos on every slide (your audience knows who you are)
- Kill template chrome, decorative borders, repeating elements
- Kill 3D charts, clip art, and "fun" transitions
- Data labels go directly on data, not in a separate legend
- The 1-7-7 Rule (one idea, seven lines max, seven words per line max) — and even that's generous

### 6. Picture Superiority Effect

> People remember 10% of what they hear, 65% of what they see and hear.

**Rule:** Show, don't tell. Visuals are not decoration — they're the primary carrier of meaning.

**In practice:**
- Full-bleed images with minimal overlaid text
- One powerful image > three mediocre ones
- Charts beat tables. Annotated charts beat raw charts. A single highlighted data point beats all of the above.
- Quotes work better as large typography on a dark/light background
- Avoid cliché stock photos (handshakes in front of globes, people staring at laptops)

### 7. Story-Driven Design

> Design serves the narrative, not the other way around.

**Rule:** You're telling a story. The visuals are your set design.

**In practice:**
- Start with the story, then design slides to support each beat
- Use contrast to signal shifts (problem → solution, before → after)
- Section/"bumper" slides give the audience landmarks
- PUNCH: Personal, Unexpected, Novel, Challenging, Humorous

## The SUCCESs Framework (from Made to Stick)

For any communication you design, verify:

- **S**imple — One core message. Ruthlessly distilled.
- **U**nexpected — Violate expectations. Open knowledge gaps, then fill them.
- **C**oncrete — Real examples, not abstractions. "Put a man on the moon" > "Maximize aerospace leadership."
- **C**redible — Contextualized data, testimonials, specific evidence.
- **E**motional — Make people feel something. Images > bullet points for emotion.
- **S**tories — Humans are wired for narrative. Wrap facts in story.

## The Analog-First Creative Process

**Never start a design on the computer.** The tool constrains your thinking.

1. **Brainstorm** — Paper, whiteboard, sticky notes. Quantity over quality. No editing yet.
2. **Group & identify the core** — Chunk similar ideas. Find the ONE key message. "What's the one thing I want them to remember?"
3. **Storyboard off-computer** — Arrange ideas in sequence. Test the flow. Add/remove/reorder.
4. **Sketch visuals** — Rough pencil sketches of how to visualize each idea. Bad drawings are fine.
5. **Go digital** — Only now open the software. Build from your storyboard.

## Anti-Patterns (The "Don't Do This" List)

- ❌ Slideuments — slides that double as handouts. Slides ≠ documents. Make a separate handout.
- ❌ Templates that fight you — company templates with heavy chrome, small content areas, logo on every slide
- ❌ Reading slides aloud — slides are visual support, not a teleprompter
- ❌ The "one more thing" trap — if it doesn't serve the core message, cut it. No matter how cool.
- ❌ Fear-driven bloat — "I'll include everything just to be safe." It's the opposite of safe.
- ❌ Corporate abstraction — "leverage synergies to maximize stakeholder value." Speak human.

## Design Review Checklist

When reviewing any visual design, ask:

1. **What's the ONE thing?** Can a viewer grasp the core message in 3 seconds?
2. **What can I remove?** Find at least 3 things and eliminate them.
3. **Does this element earn its place?** If it doesn't directly support the message, cut it.
4. **Is there room to breathe?** Increase white space by 50%.
5. **Is this concrete?** Replace abstractions with examples, images, or data with context.
6. **Does it feel natural?** Kill anything that feels forced, templated, or "designed."
7. **What's the story?** Is there a clear beginning, middle, and end?
8. **Would *I* pay attention to this?** Be honest.
