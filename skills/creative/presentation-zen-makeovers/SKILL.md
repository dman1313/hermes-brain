# Presentation Zen Makeovers — The Complete Design Methodology

Sourced from Garr Reynolds' [slide-makeovers](https://www.garrreynolds.com/slide-makeovers) and [design-tips](https://www.garrreynolds.com/design-tips) pages. This is the practical, tactical skill — for the philosophical foundations, see the `zen-design-principles` skill.

## Core Philosophy

Three words govern all design:

- **Restraint** in preparation
- **Simplicity** in design
- **Naturalness** in delivery

> "Fear is the path to the dark side. Fear leads to bullet points. Bullet points lead to boredom. And boredom leads to suffering." — Master Yoda (via Garr Reynolds)

> "Making the simple complicated is commonplace; making the complicated simple, awesomely simple, that's creativity." — Charles Mingus

> "Technical knowledge is not enough. One must transcend techniques so that the art becomes an artless art, growing out of the unconscious." — Daisetsu Suzuki

## The 14 Design Rules

### 1. Keep It Simple

> "What can be done with fewer is done in vain with more." — Edward Tufte

- Nothing in your visual should be superfluous
- Slides should have plenty of empty/white/negative space
- Do not fill empty areas with logos, graphics, or text boxes that don't contribute
- The less clutter, the more powerful the message
- One idea per slide/view

### 2. Make It Visual

**Picture Superiority Effect:** Pictures are remembered better than words — especially for casual, time-limited exposure. After 30+ seconds, recall for pictures far exceeds recall for words.

- Information best recalled when pictures and words reinforce the same message
- Effect is strongest with common, concrete things
- Visual imagery is a powerful mnemonic tool
- Modern presentations share more with cinema and comics than written documents
- **Free image sources:** Pexels.com, Unsplash.com, Pixabay.com, Freeimages.com, Stocksnap.io, Kaboompics.com, Burst.shopify.com, Reshot.com, Lifeofpics.com, Barnimages.com
- **Paid image sources:** Adobe Stock, Shutterstock, iStockphoto, Storyblocks, Canva

### 3. Don't Create "Slideuments"

> Slides are slides. Documents are documents. They aren't the same thing.

- A "slideument" = slide + document → serves neither purpose
- Projected slides should be as visual as possible, supporting speech
- Verbal content, proof, evidence, appeal comes from the spoken word
- **The best slides may have no text at all** — meaningless without your narration
- If someone asks for your slides after, they should be of little use without you
- Instead: prepare a written document OR make a video of the presentation

### 4. Aim for a High Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)

> "The ratio of relevant to irrelevant information in a display. The highest possible SNR is desirable." — Universal Principles of Design

- Every element should be expressed to the extent necessary, but not beyond
- Degradation sources: inappropriate charts, ambiguous labels, unnecessary emphasis
- **If an item can be removed without compromising function, remove it**
- Grid lines can often be lightened, thinned, or removed
- Footer logos and decorations can usually be removed entirely

### 5. Design for the Back of the Room

> The problem isn't that text is too big — it's that it's way too small.

- Make text large enough for the person in the very last row
- Fill the screen; design elements must be easy to see
- Presentations are a "glance media" — closer to billboards than documents
- **Ask: Can the message be processed effectively within 3 seconds?** — Nancy Duarte
- Applies equally to video/online presentations viewed on small screens

### 6. Make Images Large — Often Fullscreen

- One large, powerful image > several smaller images on the same slide
- Place text on top in an area with good empty space
- Full-bleed images with type overlay
- Exception: side-by-side is appropriate for Before/After or Then/Now comparisons
- Even when using multiple images, make them as large as possible

### 7. Use Contrast to Highlight and Make Visuals Clear

> We are wired to notice differences. Contrast is what gives design its energy.

- Make elements that are NOT the same clearly DIFFERENT
- Achieve contrast through:
  - Space (near/far, empty/filled)
  - Color (dark/light, cool/warm)
  - Typeface (serif/sans-serif, bold/narrow)
  - Positioning (top/bottom, isolated/grouped)
- Every good design has a strong, clear focal point
- **One element must be clearly dominant**
- Weak contrast = boring, hard to see, confusing

### 8. Use the Rule of Thirds

> Derived from the Golden Mean. Creates balance, beauty, and aesthetic quality.

- Divide the canvas into 9 equal boxes (3x3 grid)
- Four intersection points = "power points"
- Place main subject at a power point, NOT the center
- Creates asymmetrical balance and interest

### 9. Use Clear Charts and Graphs

Three principles for data display: **Restrain, Reduce, Emphasize.**

**Restrain:**
- Edit yourself — stop adding more
- Include as much as necessary, but no more
- Remove footers, logos, decorative items

**Reduce:**
- Ask: What will this really show? How will it help them see the point?
- What is the essence this chart helps make?
- Are any elements nonessential?

**Emphasize:**
- Where do you want eyes to go first?
- Use contrast (color) to highlight the key part
- Write a **declarative statement** as the title, not a label
  - Good: "Cases decreased by 17% in 2021"
  - Bad: "Reported Cases (2021)"

### 10. Choose Fonts Well — Keep Simple, Make Large

- **Sans-serif is generally best for projected slides** (like road signs/billboards)
- Serif typefaces can work at large sizes (Garamond, Rockwell slab serif)
- Adjust character spacing (tighter) when type is very large
- Adjust line spacing when type is large
- **The main thing: large enough to understand in an instant**

SERIF EXAMPLES: Garamond, Caslon, Minion Pro, Palatino  
SANS-SERIF EXAMPLES: Futura, Helvetica, Gil Sans, Frutiger  
SLAB SERIF: Rockwell

### 11. Use Short Video Clips

- Video promotes active cognitive processing — the natural way people learn
- Use video to show concrete examples
- Serves as a change of pace, increasing audience interest
- Embed video at 1080p, full screen when possible
- You can narrate over silent video in real-time (B-roll technique)
- **Avoid cheesy sound effects** (applause transitions, horns, etc.)

### 12. Use Colors with Purpose — Choose Just a Few

> Color is emotional. The right color can help persuade and motivate.

- **Cool colors** (blue, green) → recede, best for backgrounds
- **Warm colors** (orange, red) → come forward, best for foreground/text
- Dark room (large hall): dark background + white/light text
- Lights on (recommended): white background + dark text
- Ambient light: dark background washes out; dark-on-light maintains intensity
- Choose only 2-3 colors + neutrals — use consistently
- Color is for attention, highlighting, guiding the eye — NOT decoration

### 13. Limit Animation and Transition Effects

> A little goes a long way. The more you use an effect, the less effective it becomes.

- Use object builds and slide transitions **judiciously**
- Subtle is best: "Wipe Left-to-Right" is better than "Fly" or "Move"
- Use no more than 2-3 different transition types
- Don't put transitions between most slides
- Movement grabs attention — use that power sparingly

### 14. Spend Time in the Slide Sorter

> The Segmenting Principle: people comprehend better when info is in small chunks.

- Get out of single-slide view → see the whole flow
- Break up one dense slide into 2-3 slides for natural flow
- Capture the gestalt — see what your audience will experience
- Identify and remove extraneous elements
- This is the 35,000-ft view of visual flow and pace

## Mayer's Multimedia Learning Principles

Three assumptions about how people process information:

1. **Dual-channels** — separate channels for visual and verbal material
2. **Limited capacity** — can only attend to a few pieces per channel at a time
3. **Active processing** — learning happens when people attend, organize, and integrate

### Key Findings:

- **Multimedia Effect:** Narration + pictures > narration alone
- **Modality Principle:** Narration > on-screen text for words
- **Redundancy Principle:** Narration + graphics > narration + graphics + text
- **Coherence Principle:** Exclude extraneous visual material

### Practical Implications:

- Presentations must be both verbal AND visual
- Too much slide info overloads cognitive systems
- Can your visuals be understood in a few seconds?
- Design and delivery must help people organize and integrate info

## The Makeover Methodology

When transforming a design from BEFORE to AFTER:

### Step 1: Identify the ONE core message
What is the single thing the audience should take away? If you can't state it in one sentence, you haven't found it yet.

### Step 2: Strip to bare essentials
Remove: template chrome, logos, decorative borders, footers, 3D effects, clip art, "fun" transitions, unnecessary grid lines, redundant labels, the legend (put labels on data directly).

### Step 3: Apply the transformation patterns

**Pattern A: Text-Heavy Slide → Visual + Minimal Text**
- Before: 7 bullet points, 80 words
- After: 1 full-bleed image + 3-5 word key phrase

**Pattern B: Dense Chart → Declarative + Highlighted Chart**
- Before: Generic title, all data equal weight, grid lines, legend
- After: Declarative headline, key data highlighted in contrasting color, grid lines removed, labels on data

**Pattern C: One Dense Slide → 2-4 Visual Slides**
- Before: Everything crammed into one slide
- After: Broken into sequential slides, each with one idea + visual

**Pattern D: Small Image → Full-Bleed Image + Type Overlay**
- Before: Small image in corner, text beside it
- After: Image fills entire canvas, text overlaid on empty area

**Pattern E: Slideument → Pure Visual**
- Before: Slide that doubles as handout — dense text, bullet points, data tables
- After: Image-dominant slide that supports the speaker; separate detailed document for handout

**Pattern F: Weak Contrast → Strong Focal Point**
- Before: All elements equal weight, no clear place to look
- After: One dominant element (size/color/position), hierarchy restored

**Pattern G: Center-Composed → Rule of Thirds**
- Before: Subject dead center
- After: Subject on a power point, breathing room on the opposite side

### Step 4: Verify against the checklist
Run every design through the checklist below before calling it done.

## Universal Design Review Checklist

Apply to slides, dashboards, UIs, web pages, documents — any visual artifact.

### The 3-Second Test
- [ ] Can a viewer grasp the core message in 3 seconds?

### The Removal Test
- [ ] What 3+ things can I remove?
- [ ] Does every single element earn its place?
- [ ] Are logos, footers, and template chrome eliminated?

### The Scale Test
- [ ] Is text large enough for the back of the room (or a small screen)?
- [ ] Are images large — fullscreen/full-bleed where possible?

### The Space Test
- [ ] Is there generous empty space? (At least 50% of canvas)
- [ ] Does the main element have room to breathe?

### The Contrast Test
- [ ] Is there ONE clearly dominant element?
- [ ] Are elements that aren't the same clearly different?
- [ ] Does color guide the eye, not decorate?

### The Data Test (if applicable)
- [ ] Is the chart title a declarative statement?
- [ ] Is the key data point visually emphasized?
- [ ] Are grid lines, legends, and nonessential marks removed?
- [ ] Are labels on the data, not in a separate legend?

### The Naturalness Test
- [ ] Does this feel inevitable, not forced?
- [ ] Would it be meaningless without narration? (If so, good — it supports, not replaces.)
- [ ] Is it free of arbitrary decoration?

### The Story Test
- [ ] Is there a clear beginning, middle, end?
- [ ] Do section breaks/bumpers give landmarks?
- [ ] In slide sorter view, does the flow feel natural?

### The Honesty Test
- [ ] Would *I* pay attention to this?
- [ ] Is this better than the "before" version?
- [ ] Have I been ruthless enough in editing?

## Quick Transformation Reference

| Problem | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Too much text | Replace with image + key phrase |
| Bullet points | Break into separate slides with visuals |
| Small images | Go full-bleed |
| Generic chart title | Write a declarative headline |
| Cluttered chart | Remove grid lines, highlight one data point |
| Weak focal point | One element must dominate (size/color) |
| Center composition | Move to Rule of Thirds power point |
| Logo everywhere | Remove — audience knows who you are |
| Template chrome | Strip to bare canvas |
| Slideument | Separate into slides + document |
| Equal-weight elements | Create clear hierarchy with contrast |
| Tiny text | Design for the back of the room |

## Anti-Patterns

- ❌ Slideuments
- ❌ Bullet-point hell
- ❌ Fear-driven bloat ("include everything just to be safe")
- ❌ 3D charts, clip art, cheesy transitions
- ❌ Logos on every slide
- ❌ Templates that fight you
- ❌ Reading slides aloud
- ❌ The "one more thing" trap
- ❌ Corporate abstraction ("leverage synergies to maximize stakeholder value")
- ❌ Small images that can't be seen
